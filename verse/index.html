When Words Multiplied

“Hoodlums punch my face
I would smite them if I could
Mortality blows”

These are the first words in Rick Riordan’s Trials of Apollo book series, nested under the Chapter 1 header of the first book: the first look into the mind of Riordan’s mortal Apollo as he experiences life on the streets of New York.

Coincidentally, a fallen god’s laments became one of my first exposures to poetry. In the fourth grade, this was one of the first times I stopped to think about the meaning of words.

After reading the first chapter, I flipped back to the first page.

Mortality blows… mortality as in death, and blows as in fighting back if he could—but also mortality as in the state of being mortal, and blows as in “it sucks…” Words can hold multiple distinct meanings at once?

A recurring joke by Riordan (a new one placed under every new chapter header) had accidentally taught me a key fact of language: it’s layered, flexible, and constantly in motion.

Since then, I’ve learned that meaning is the only thing that matters in poetry. Syntactical rules still exist, of course, depending on a poem’s intended effect, but when done well, a poem can still be stellar with those rules defenestrated.

Apollo’s fall from godhood taught him what it meant to be mortal. A single line of poetry taught me what it meant for words to mean.
